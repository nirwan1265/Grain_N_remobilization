\begin{abstract}
Grain nitrogen accumulation in maize reflects coordinated uptake, assimilation, transport, storage, and remobilization of nitrogen into free and protein-bound amino acid pools. Yet the genetic links between environmental nitrogen adaptation and grain amino acid composition remain unclear. We combined amino acid profiling, genome-wide association studies (GWAS), environmental GWAS, biparental QTL mapping, and recurrent-selection population genomics to dissect grain nitrogen metabolism across complementary genetic contexts. In the Goodman–Buckler Association Panel, mature grain free amino acid (FAA) composition was dominated by asparagine and proline. GWAS of amino acid traits identified loci tied to amino acid transport, Glu/Gln metabolism, Asp-family metabolism, redox regulation, and protein turnover. In a georeferenced maize landrace panel, soil-N GWAS highlighted loci including an omega-amidase/NIT2-like CN hydrolase and an aspartate/glutamate/uridylate kinase-domain gene, linking historical soil nitrogen to Asn/Gln and Asp-family metabolism. 
To connect these signals with selection-driven nitrogen partitioning, we profiled Indian Chief and Jarvis populations after 14 cycles of yield selection that produced contrasting grain and stover N dynamics. Indian Chief Cycle 14 showed a marked expansion of the FAA pool, increased FAA-derived nitrogen, and strong proline enrichment with reduced Asp and Glu fractions, whereas Jarvis showed weaker changes, including a nominal increase in Asp fraction. Low-pass sequencing and population-genomic scans revealed population-specific differentiation at loci annotated for amino acid transport, autophagy, ubiquitin–proteasome activity, proteolysis, Glu/Gln cycling, and Asp-family metabolism. B64 × CML14 biparental mapping identified a shared chromosome 3 QTL for Pro and Asp, implicating endosperm protein trafficking via a SAR1A candidate near the QTL peak. Collectively, these results support a model in which maize grain nitrogen variation arises not from a single conserved locus but from repeated modulation of interconnected pathways controlling Asp-derived nitrogen flux, Glu/Gln-mediated C–N recycling, protein turnover, and intracellular transport during kernel development.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

\lettrine[lines=2]{\color{color2}N}{itrogen} (N) is essential for maize growth, specifically kernel development, but its use in agriculture presents a major sustainability challenge. Modern crop production relies heavily on N fertilizer, yet much of the applied N is not recovered by crops and is lost via leaching, runoff, or gaseous emissions (ref). Improving nitrogen use efficiency (NUE) therefore requires understanding how plants acquire inorganic N from soil, assimilate it into organic compounds, transport it among tissues, and remobilize it into developing grain \citep{MasclauxDaubresse2010NUE,Have2017NRemobilization}. In maize kernels, N is stored mainly in amino acid pools, as either free amino acids (FAAs) or protein-bound amino acids (PBAAs), making grain amino acid composition a direct biochemical readout of grain N allocation.

Plant N metabolism incorporates N uptake, assimilation, transport, and recycling. Nitrate and ammonium are assimilated primarily via the GS/GOGAT cycle, which incorporates ammonium into glutamine and regenerates glutamate using 2-oxoglutarate as a carbon skeleton \citep{MasclauxDaubresse2010NUE,Hodges2002TwoOxoglutarate}. Thus, Glu/Gln metabolism lies at the interface of N assimilation and central carbon metabolism. Asparagine and glutamine are major N-rich transport and storage compounds, while glutamate and aspartate act as central amino-group donors and C–N metabolic hubs \citep{Bernard2009CytosolicGS,MasclauxDaubresse2010NUE}. Aspartate is especially important because it feeds the aspartate-family pathway, which produces methionine, threonine, lysine, and isoleucine, and also connects to asparagine, NAD, and nucleotide metabolism \citep{Viola2001AspartatePathway,Wang2018AspSeeds,Han2021Aspartate}.

Grain N accumulation also depends on N remobilization from vegetative tissues during reproduction. Leaf senescence is a regulated process in which chloroplast proteins, nucleic acids, and other N-rich macromolecules are degraded and exported to developing sinks \citep{Have2017NRemobilization,Sakuraba2022NStarvationSenescence}. Protein breakdown proceeds via chloroplast degradation, vacuolar and autophagic pathways, and the ubiquitin--26S proteasome system \citep{Liu2008ProteinDegradationNRemobilization,Have2017NRemobilization}. The resulting amino acids and ammonium are converted into mobile N forms, transported through source--sink pathways, and stored in grains. Cytosolic glutamine synthetase is central to this recycling because it reassimilates ammonium into glutamine, a major mobile organic N form \citep{Bernard2009CytosolicGS}. Proline adds a stress-linked amino-acid axis. In maize, water stress can modify proline accumulation or export from senescing leaves in a genotype-specific way, while glutamine and asparagine remain the main N compounds in leaf exudates \citep{Carceller1999ProlineRemobilization}. Therefore, changes in grain FAA composition should be interpreted as remodeling across multiple N-related amino-acid pools, including Asn/Gln transport forms, Asp/Glu C--N metabolic hubs, and Pro linked metabolism, rather than as direct evidence of increased total N remobilization alone.


Although N uptake, amino-acid metabolism, and senescence-associated remobilization are well studied, they are usually examined separately. It remains unclear whether the same N-metabolism functions recur across long-term environmental adaptation, natural variation in grain amino-acid composition, and short-term selection for contrasting grain–stover N partitioning. Maize is well suited for such integrative analysis. The Goodman–Buckler Association Panel captures broad allelic diversity for mapping complex traits \citep{FlintGarcia2005Goodman}, georeferenced landrace panels enable tests of genetic associations with environmental gradients such as soil N \citep{RomeroNavarro2017Landraces}, and the Indian Chief and Jarvis populations add a selection layer, as 14 cycles of reciprocal recurrent selection for grain yield produced contrasting N-remobilization patterns in grains and stover \citep{Moll1994RecurrentSelection}.

We integrate grain amino-acid profiling, grain amino-acid GWAS, soil-N environmental GWAS, population-genomic scans of Indian Chief and Jarvis, and an exploratory B64 × CML14 biparental mapping population developed for grain asparagine variation. We analyze free and protein-bound amino-acid pools, focusing on asparagine, aspartate, glutamate, glutamine, and proline because of their roles in N transport, C–N recycling, amino-acid storage, and stress-linked N metabolism. Across these layers, we test whether genetic, and selection signals converge on shared processes underlying grain N remodeling. Our results support a model in which maize grain N variation repeatedly involves interconnected pathways controlling Asp-family metabolism, Glu/Gln-mediated C–N recycling, amino-acid transport, protein turnover, and intracellular trafficking during kernel development.






















\section{Results}\label{results}

\subsection{Kernel FAA variation in the Goodman--Buckler panel highlights Asn/Pro dominance and N-related GWAS signals}

Free amino acid (FAA) composition was evaluated across 280 maize lines grown in four replicates at Genetics Farm near Columbia, Missouri (Supplementary Table 1). To assess relative abundance, amino acids were ranked within each genotype based on average values across replicates. Asparagine and proline consistently showed the highest relative abundance across the panel (Supplementary Fig. \ref{fig:supp_faa_asn_pro_rank} A--B). Asparagine was ranked as the most abundant amino acid in 144 of 280 genotypes (51\%), while proline ranked first in 116 genotypes (41\%). Notably, asparagine and proline comprised the top two amino acids in 180 of 280 genotypes (64\%), regardless of order (Supplementary Table 1). 

Furthermore, to identify loci associated with grain amino acid composition, we analyzed 78 amino acid-derived phenotypes (Supplementary Tables 1-2). The phenotypes were based on their importance for N metabolism. At a significance threshold of $-\log_{10}(p) \geq 7$, we identified 1,941 unique candidate genes. Most candidate genes were associated with a single phenotype (1,646 genes), whereas 295 genes were shared across multiple phenotypes. The largest number of candidate genes was detected for \textit{N.E} with 76 genes. The amino-acid-derived N proxy traits (define proxy N), defined as \textit{Total\_N} and \textit{Total\_PBAA} were associated with 26 and 24 candidate genes, respectively. Among annotated significant hits, associations were dominated by FarmCPU and BLINK. The complete GWAS results are available through the Shiny web server at:
\url{https://j83gk2-nirwan-tandukar.shinyapps.io/amino_gwas_app/}.

For downstream interpretation, we focused on traits related to nitrogen assimilation and redistribution, including absolute concentrations of asparagine (\textit{N}), aspartate (\textit{D}), glutamate (\textit{E}), glutamine (\textit{Q}), and proline (\textit{P}); related family and ratio traits (\textit{IMNTDK}, \textit{D.IMNTDK}, \textit{N.E}, \textit{N.Total}, \textit{Q.E}); and proxy traits \textit{Total\_N} and \textit{Total\_PBAA}. Gene annotations for this subset supported a nitrogen-centered amino acid remodeling signal. Absolute glutamine (\textit{Q}) was associated with a \textit{NRT1/PTR FAMILY 3.1} transporter (\textit{Zm00001eb287980}), whereas the aspartate-family composite trait \textit{D.IMNTDK} mapped to a \textit{NRT1/PTR FAMILY 4.6} transporter (\textit{Zm00001eb277160}) and a tyrosine aminotransferase (\textit{Zm00001eb201770}) annotated with L-tyrosine:2-oxoglutarate transaminase activity, linking amino acid homeostasis to 2-oxoglutarate-dependent transamination. Glutamate (\textit{E}) was associated with \textit{AMINO ACID TRANSPORTER AVT3A} (\textit{Zm00001eb429100}), and \textit{Total\_N} included a \textit{GLUTAMATE DEHYDROGENASE 3}-related locus (\textit{Zm00001eb060520}) annotated with amino acid metabolism, L-glutamate catabolism, and response to nitrogen starvation. Within the same \textit{Total\_N} interval, we identified a glutathione S-transferase gene cluster (\textit{Zm00001eb021640}, \textit{Zm00001eb021650}, \textit{Zm00001eb021690}) that was also associated with \textit{Total\_PBAA}, suggesting convergence between proxy nitrogen content and protein-bound amino acid composition.

Candidate annotations also pointed to senescence, proteostasis, and protein turnover. In the aspartate (\textit{D}) GWAS, significant loci mapped to or near a RING-type E3 ubiquitin ligase (\textit{Zm00001eb293380}) and a Derlin-1-like component of ER-associated degradation (ERAD; \textit{Zm00001eb283510}). The \textit{D.IMNTDK} trait encompassed multiple ubiquitin/proteolysis-related loci, including SKP1-like and deubiquitinase genes and a candidate annotated for autophagosome assembly (\textit{Zm00001eb429920}). The broader \textit{Total} trait included an EI24-like macroautophagy gene (\textit{Zm00001eb082660}) and a putative senescence regulator (\textit{Zm00001eb322660}), suggesting that grain amino acid profiles may also be shaped by protein recycling and senescence-associated nutrient remobilization.

% Together, these results indicate that grain amino acid GWAS supports a broader model in which grain N-related amino acid composition reflects coordinated variation in N assimilation, amino acid interconversion, transport, and proteostasis.

Furthermore, to place FAA composition in the broader grain amino-acid pool, we compared genotype-level FAA and PBAA across the Goodman--Buckler panel using matched taxa (Supplementary Fig.~\ref{fig:supp_gb_faa_pbaa_metrics}; Supplementary Tables~12--14). The protein-bound pool was much larger than the free pool for both total amino acids and amino-acid-derived N. In contrast, FAA was more enriched for proline and had a higher N-rich fraction than PBAA, showing that FAA and PBAA capture distinct but complementary aspects of grain N allocation. Thus, the free pool was not simply a scaled-down version of the protein-bound pool but instead, FAA was biased toward specific amino-acid classes, including proline, suggesting that variation in FAA composition reflects active amino-acid pool remodeling rather than only differences in total grain amino-acid abundance.


\subsection{Soil nitrogen enviornment GWAS highlights aspartate-family and asparagine metabolism in maize landrace adaptation}

The Goodman-Buckler amino acid GWAS results suggested that grain N-related amino acid variation is associated with amino acid transport, Glu/Gln metabolism, Asp-family metabolism, and protein turnover. We therefore asked whether natural variation in maize landraces exposed to different soil N environments pointed to similar biological pathways. The soil N GWAS reflects historical environmental associations rather than a directly measured grain phenotype (Ref soil N map). Therefore, we interpreted this comparison cautiously. Our objective was not to equate soil N adaptation with grain amino acid composition, but rather to assess whether independent ecological and grain-biochemical layers converged on related N-metabolism functions. To address this, we integrated the Romero Navarro \citep{RomeroNavarro2017Landraces} maize landrace panel with total soil N estimates for each accession (ref). The sampled landraces span Mexico, Central America, and South America, capturing broad variation in soil N availability (Fig.~\ref{fig:soilN_gwas}A).

Furthermore, we performed GWAS using a mixed linear model with three genotype principal components as covariates, and also applied the same models as in the grain GWAS, summarizing the strongest signals across the models (Fig.~\ref{fig:soilN_gwas}B). The GWAS identified multiple loci exceeding the Bonferroni significance thresholds (Supplementary Fig.~\ref{fig:supp_soiln_qq_venn} A). Model overlap analysis showed that BLINK and FarmCPU yielded most of the high-confidence signals, with 79 SNPs shared between them and six SNPs shared across all four models (Supplementary Fig.~\ref{fig:supp_soiln_qq_venn} B).

Two of the six SNPs shared across all four GWAS models mapped to focal nitrogen-metabolism candidate loci, S1\_16128632 within \textit{Zm00001eb005710} and S5\_22338979 4.4 kb downstream of \textit{Zm00001eb220040}. \textit{Zm00001eb005710}, encodes a CN hydrolase domain-containing protein. It is annotated as omega-amidase/NIT2-like activity, hydrolase activity on C--N bonds, and roles in oxaloacetate, asparagine, and glutamine metabolism (Supplementary Table 3). A second candidate, \textit{Zm00001eb220040}, encodes a protein with an aspartate/glutamate/uridylate kinase domain. Its GO annotations include aspartate kinase activity, amino acid biosynthesis, and threonine biosynthesis. Since aspartate kinase catalyzes an early committed step in the aspartate-family amino acid pathway, this locus is a plausible mediator of soil N effects on downstream amino acid biosynthesis (Supplementary Table 3). These inferences are based on annotations and GO terms rather than direct experiments and should be viewed cautiously.

Two of the loci point to regulatory and trafficking mechanisms. SNP S3\_54622531 lies within 25 kb of \textit{Zm00001eb130210}, a RING/FYVE/PHD zinc-finger and ubiquitin ligase whose rice homolog \textit{TTL1}/\textit{OsRFPV-7} regulates thermotolerance and grain size, with loss-of-function alleles increasing both \citep{Lin2023TTL1}. A second SNP, S9\_129109559, falls within \textit{Zm00001eb393130}, a vacuolar protein sorting-associated protein 52A gene, with a nearby nitrilase-associated gene, \textit{Zm00001eb393120}, in the same 25-kb window. Arabidopsis Vps52p/POK localizes to the Golgi and functions in vesicle trafficking and polar growth \citep{Lobstein2004Vps52}, suggesting this locus may link soil-N association to protein sorting, vesicle trafficking, and intracellular transport that could alter nutrient redistribution or cellular remodeling under contrasting N conditions. Together with the omega-amidase and aspartate kinase loci, these SNPs indicate that the shared soil-N GWAS signal includes both direct N-metabolism candidates and broader regulatory or trafficking components.

\subsection{Free amino acid analysis in Indian Chief and Jarvis implicate protein turnover in population-specific N allocation}

To connect the Buckler-Goodman amino acid GWAS and soil N environment GWAS with a selection-based population framework, we next examined the Indian Chief and Jarvis maize populations. These populations provide an independent system for testing whether short-term selection altered amino acid pools and N allocation in grains in the same way because Indian Chief and Jarvis served as open-pollinated base populations in a reciprocal recurrent selection experiment for grain weight over 14 cycles \citep{Moll1994}. The authors showed that reciprocal recurrent selection altered N allocation differently. At nonlimiting N supply, Jarvis, after 14 cycles of reciprocal recurrent selection, accumulated greater grain N and exhibited greater post-anthesis loss of N from stover relative to the starting Jarvis population. In contrast, Indian Chief, after 14 cycles of reciprocal recurrent selection, showed increased post-anthesis stover biomass but had lower grain weight, lower post-anthesis loss of N from stover, and no significant increase in grain N relative to the original Indian Chief population \citep{Moll1994}.

For FAA profiling, representative Cycle~0 and Cycle~14 individuals from Indian Chief and Jarvis were grown at the Central Crops Research Station in Clayton, North Carolina, under standard field management practices. We then analyzed FAA  abundance and composition metrics at Cycle~0 and Cycle~14 for grains (Fig. \ref{fig:fig4_faa}; Supplementary Fig.~\ref{fig:supp_pbaa_profiles}; Supplementary Table 5). Indian Chief showed significant remodeling of both total FAA abundance and relative FAA composition. Total FAA abundance increased from 7.62 in Cycle~0 to 13.42 in Cycle~14 (Wilcoxon rank-sum $p = 0.0043$; BH-adjusted $p = 0.0194$), indicating an expansion of the measured free amino acid pool. Within this expanded pool, proline became more dominant (proline fraction increased from 0.320 to 0.519 ($p = 0.0157$; BH-adjusted $p = 0.0283$)). In contrast, acidic amino acids decreased as fractions of the total FAA pool. The aspartate fraction decreased from 0.115 to 0.075 ($p = 0.0157$; BH-adjusted $p = 0.0283$), and the glutamate fraction decreased from 0.140 to 0.062 ($p = 0.0068$; BH-adjusted $p = 0.0203$) (Supplementary Table 4). Thus, Indian Chief Cycle 14 showed both increased total FAA abundance and a compositional shift away from Asp/Glu fractions and toward proline enrichment. This shift is relevant because the soil N GWAS identified candidate genes connected to aspartate/asparagine and glutamine metabolism, including an aspartate kinase candidate.

In contrast, Jarvis showed fewer significant changes. The clearest Jarvis signal was an increase in Asp fraction from 0.075 in Cycle~0 to 0.137 in Cycle~14. This change was nominally significant before multiple-testing correction (Wilcoxon rank-sum $p = 0.0189$) but not after BH correction ($p = 0.1323$). Other FAA metrics (total FAA abundance, proline fraction, N-rich FAA pool, and Glu fraction) did not differ significantly between cycles (Supplementary Table 5). Thus, unlike Indian Chief, Jarvis did not show statistically supported changes.


To test whether FAA compositional remodeling was accompanied by changes in amino acid-derived N pools, we converted FAA measurements to N-equivalent values by weighting each amino acid by its N content and normalizing by sample mass (Supplementary Fig.~\ref{fig:supp_ic_j_aa_n_pools}; Supplementary Table 6). These values represent amino acid-derived N pools, and not total elemental grain N as in the original study \citep{Moll1994}. In Indian Chief, FAA-derived N per mg increased significantly from Cycle~0 to Cycle~14 (1.75 to 2.99; Wilcoxon rank-sum $p = 0.0043$; BH-adjusted $p = 0.0129$), indicating the expansion of the free amino acid N pool. Other changes were not significant. Thus, amino acid-derived N estimates indicate a significant increase in the free amino acid N pool in Indian Chief Cycle~14, whereas Jarvis showed only nonsignificant decreases. 

\subsection{Skim sequencing reveals generation-specific genetic differentiation in Indian Chief and Jarvis}

We also performed skim sequencing on approximately 100 individuals each from the Indian Chief and Jarvis maize populations for both cycles. Sample-level sequencing metrics matched expectations for low-coverage skim sequencing (Supplementary Fig.~\ref{fig:supp_seq_sample}). Across 379 individuals, the mean sequencing depth was 0.73$\times$ (median 0.72$\times$, range 0.32--1.91$\times$). Most samples had 0.5--1.0$\times$ coverage (323/379); 26 were below 0.5$\times$, 29 were between 1.0$\times$ and 1.5$\times$, and one exceeded 1.5$\times$ (Supplementary Fig.~\ref{fig:supp_seq_sample} A,C). Despite the low depth, genotype recovery was high and uniform, with $\sim$59.0 million genotypes called per individual (Supplementary Fig.~\ref{fig:supp_seq_sample} B). Missing data were moderate for skim sequencing, with a median of 102{,}814 missing genotypes per sample.

Variant-level summary statistics indicated that the dataset was suitable for population-level genetic analyses (Supplementary Fig.~\ref{fig:supp_seq_variant}). Per-site sequencing depth was centered near 7$\times$ across retained variants, with an interquartile range of 5–9$\times$ (Supplementary Fig.~\ref{fig:supp_seq_variant} A). The minor allele frequency spectrum was skewed toward low-frequency variants, as expected for segregating breeding populations with substantial standing genetic variation (Supplementary Fig.~\ref{fig:supp_seq_variant} B) (ref). Missing genotype counts declined smoothly across samples ranked by missingness, indicating that missing data were not driven by a small subset of problematic individuals (Supplementary Fig.~\ref{fig:supp_seq_variant} C). Transition/transversion ratios were highly consistent across the 10 maize chromosomes (1.74–1.81; genome-wide mean 1.785; Supplementary Fig.~\ref{fig:supp_seq_variant} D). After filtering, 512,667 SNPs were retained, and a linkage-disequilibrium–pruned subset of 57,568 markers was used for population-structure analyses.

Population-structure analyses showed that Indian Chief and Jarvis remained genetically differentiated after 14 generations of selection, while also exhibiting clear generation-associated shifts within each population (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig3}A). Individual ancestry profiles clustered primarily by source population, with little evidence of extensive admixture. Within each population, comparisons between Cycle~0 and Cycle~14 revealed shifts in inferred ancestry components, consistent with selection-driven allele-frequency changes. PCA separated the four population--cycle groups, with PC1 primarily distinguishing Indian Chief from Jarvis and PC2 separating Cycle~0 from Cycle~14 within each population (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig3}B). Thus, long-term selection preserved the ancestral population identities of Indian Chief and Jarvis while generating detectable genome-wide differentiation between ancestral and selected generations.

Shared-SNP summaries provide a complementary view of allele-composition shifts across cycles (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig3}C,D). This metric is directional, as it measures the proportion of SNPs observed in one group that are also observed in the comparison group. Within each population, a high proportion of Cycle~0 SNPs were also observed in Cycle~14, whereas a lower proportion of Cycle~14 SNPs were shared back with Cycle~0. This asymmetry suggests that many ancestral alleles persisted after selection, whereas Cycle~14 individuals also carried cycle-enriched alleles that were less common in Cycle~0 (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig3}C). Between populations, Cycle~14 individuals showed greater SNP sharing between Indian Chief and Jarvis than Cycle~0 individuals (mean shared proportion $\sim$0.742 vs.\ 0.420; Fig.~\ref{fig:fig3}D). Thus, reciprocal recurrent selection reshaped allele frequencies within each population and increased allelic overlap between the Cycle~14 populations, even though structure and PCA analyses indicated that Indian Chief and Jarvis remained genetically distinct.

\subsection{Selection scans reveal population-specific amino acid and protein-turnover candidates}

To identify genomic regions differentiated across 14 cycles of reciprocal recurrent selection, we compared Cycle~0 and Cycle~14 within Indian Chief and Jarvis using three population-genetic statistics: $F_{ST}$, $\Delta$ Tajima's~$D$, and $\log_2(\pi_{C14}/\pi_{C0})$ (Fig. \ref{fig:fig5}). Genome-wide scans included 2,162 windows in Indian Chief and 2,151 in Jarvis (Supplementary Table 7,8). We prioritized a conservative concordant set requiring windows to fall in the top 5\% of $F_{ST}$ and the bottom 5\% of both $\Delta$ Tajima's~$D$ and $\log_2(\pi_{C14}/\pi_{C0})$. This filter identified 8 windows in Indian Chief and 10 in Jarvis, containing 213 and 184 genes respectively. We treated these as higher-confidence candidates because they showed coordinated signals of differentiation and reduced diversity after selection.


In Indian Chief, concordant candidate regions were enriched for functions related to protein turnover, cellular recycling, nitrogen transport, and amino acid metabolism (Supplementary Table 9). These included autophagy-related genes, ubiquitin and proteasome components, cysteine and aspartyl proteases, and transporters involved in ammonium or peptide/nitrate transport (ref). Representative candidates included \textit{Zm00001eb372060}, \textit{Zm00001eb287690}, and \textit{Zm00001eb352300}, annotated as autophagy-related proteins; \textit{Zm00001eb214780}, annotated as a senescence-specific cysteine protease; \textit{Zm00001eb214890}, annotated as an ammonium transporter; \textit{Zm00001eb287950}, annotated as an NRT1/PTR-family transporter; and \textit{Zm00001eb214770}, annotated as NADP-specific glutamate dehydrogenase. These functions are consistent with cellular protein degradation, organelle recycling, and amino acid release during nutrient redistribution.

Since reciprocal recurrent selection was applied for only 14 cycles, selected loci may show moderate allele-frequency shifts without strong sweep-like reductions in diversity or consistent changes in Tajima's~$D$. We therefore also examined the broader top 5\% $F_{ST}$ outlier set (Supplementary Table 7). They were used as an exploratory set to evaluate whether selection cycles converged with independent evidence from grain FAA GWAS and soil N GWAS. Using this broader $F_{ST}$-only scan, the Indian Chief candidate set reinforced the amino acid and N-remobilization themes identified by the concordant scan. Candidate genes included \textit{Zm00001eb214360}, annotated as aspartate-semialdehyde dehydrogenase; \textit{Zm00001eb294790}, annotated as threonine synthase; \textit{Zm00001eb060520}, \textit{Zm00001eb427810}, and \textit{Zm00001eb162300}, annotated as glutamate dehydrogenase or glutamate dehydrogenase-related genes; \textit{Zm00001eb162900}, annotated as glutamine synthetase; and \textit{Zm00001eb217220}, annotated as a glutamine amidotransferase domain-containing protein. Indian Chief also contained amino acid transporter candidates and a glutaredoxin-family candidate. Together, these annotations suggest that selection-associated differentiation in Indian Chief involved broader remodeling of Asp-family metabolism, Glu/Gln interconversion, amino acid transport, protein turnover, and redox-associated N metabolism. This genomic pattern matches the FAA phenotype in Indian Chief Cycle~14, where total FAA abundance and Pro fraction increased while Asp and Glu fractions decreased.

In Jarvis, concordant candidate regions also contained genes related to protein turnover, translation, organellar function, and stress-associated metabolism. Additional candidates were associated with ER stress, chloroplast protein import, thylakoid organization, and mitochondrial electron transport. These annotations suggest that Jarvis differentiation also involved protein quality control and organellar remodeling.

Similarly, the broader Jarvis $F_{ST}$ outlier set revealed a stronger population-specific connection to Asp-family amino acid metabolism (Supplementary Table 8). Most notably, Jarvis contained \textit{Zm00001eb094670}, annotated as aspartate kinase with GO terms for aspartate kinase activity and biosynthesis of threonine, lysine via diaminopimelate, and homoserine. This candidate is highly relevant as the Asp fraction showed the clearest FAA compositional change in Jarvis, increasing from Cycle~0 to Cycle~14 before multiple-testing correction. The presence of an aspartate kinase candidate provides a direct genomic link between the Jarvis $F_{ST}$ scan and Asp-family metabolism. Additional Jarvis candidates supported amino acid transport and interconversion, including \textit{Zm00001eb231430} (aspartate--tRNA ligase), \textit{Zm00001eb237500} (D-amino-acid transaminase), \textit{Zm00001eb132750} (lysine/histidine transporter-like protein), \textit{Zm00001eb159580} (putative GABA transporter), and \textit{Zm00001eb389560} (GLUTAMINE DUMPER 5). Jarvis also contained glutamine/glutathione-related candidates, including \textit{Zm00001eb232460}, \textit{Zm00001eb131830}, \textit{Zm00001eb131840}, and \textit{Zm00001eb210730}. Thus, Jarvis selection-associated differentiation appears to involve Asp-family metabolism, amino acid transport, glutamine-linked metabolism, and glutathione-associated redox processes.


\subsection{B64 $\times$ CML14 biparental mapping identifies a shared chromosome 3 QTL for Asp and Pro}

We used a previously developed B64 $\times$ CML14 F$_{2:3}$ biparental population that had been used to map kernel free asparagine content \citep{FitzsimmonsInPrep}. Although this population was originally selected for variation in asparagine rather than proline or aspartate, grain amino-acid measurements for the same lines allowed us to test whether other amino-acid traits also showed segregating genetic variation in this background. B64 and CML14 were not the most extreme lines for Pro and Asp in the Goodman–Buckler panel (Supplementary Fig. \ref{fig:supp_ic_j_aa_n_pools}). Thus, we treated this biparental family as an exploratory test of segregating amino-acid variation. We focused on Pro and Asp because our other population data implicated these traits. Asp-family metabolism repeatedly emerged in the soil N GWAS and Indian Chief/Jarvis selection scans, while proline was strongly remodeled in Indian Chief and overlapped with recurrent-selection candidate regions.

BLUPs for free Pro and Asp were calculated across two replicates and used for QTL mapping. Interval-mapping QTL scans were performed using Haley--Knott regression. The QTL scans for Pro and Asp identified the same QTL, while the three Asn traits identified another QTL (Fig. \ref{fig:fig6_qtl_pro_asp}). The genetic map consisted of 611 SNP markers (Fig. \ref{fig:fig6_qtl_pro_asp}) distributed across 10 linkage groups, and the significance threshold was determined after 1,000 permutations ($\alpha = 0.05$).

\section{Discussion}\label{sec12}

The results support a model in which maize grain N variation arises through multiple genetic pathways that converge on related biological functions. Across soil-N environmental GWAS, grain amino-acid GWAS, Indian Chief and Jarvis population-genomic scans, and exploratory B64 $\times$ CML14 biparental mapping, most candidate genes were specific to individual datasets, yet their annotations consistently involved connected N-remobilization and amino-acid metabolism processes. We therefore interpret the study as evidence for functional rather than single-locus convergence. Candidate genes supporting this view are listed in Supplementary Table S11, and the conceptual model is shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:fig7_integrative_synthesis}. The following sections examine the main recurring functional modules and their implications for grain amino-acid composition and N allocation.

\subsection{Aspartate-family metabolism recurrently links soil-N adaptation, recurrent selection, and grain amino-acid variation}

A central theme of this study is the repeated involvement of aspartate-family metabolism across independent analyses. Instead of implicating the same gene in every population or dataset, our results highlight different enzymes within a shared metabolic network. Thus, the signal reflects pathway-level convergence rather than single-locus convergence. Aspartate is well suited to this role because it is both a proteinogenic amino acid and a N-bearing metabolic hub, connecting carbon skeletons with amino-group transfer, NAD and nucleotide metabolism, and the biosynthesis of methionine, threonine, lysine, and isoleucine \citep{Han2021Aspartate,Wang2018AspSeeds,Viola2001AspartatePathway}.

The recurrent-selection populations provided the clearest population-specific signal for this pathway. In Jarvis, the broader $F_{ST}$ outlier set identified \textit{Zm00001eb094670}, annotated as \textit{ask2}/aspartate kinase, which catalyzes the first committed step of the aspartate-family pathway and directs aspartate toward aspartyl phosphate and downstream methionine, threonine, lysine, and isoleucine biosynthesis \citep{Viola2001AspartatePathway,Wang2018AspSeeds}. The connection to grain amino-acid composition is supported by maize \textit{ask2}, which has been linked to methionine accumulation in developing kernels \citep{Duo2026Ask2}. Thus, the Jarvis \textit{ask2} candidate links selection-associated genomic differentiation to the allocation of aspartate-derived N into grain amino-acid pools.

Jarvis also included additional aspartate-related candidates. \textit{Zm00001eb152450}, annotated as an aspartate aminotransferase/glutamate--oxaloacetate transaminase gene, mediates a transamination step linking aspartate, oxaloacetate, glutamate, and 2-oxoglutarate. This reaction connects aspartate metabolism to central C--N balance and can shift the relative sizes of Asp and Glu pools \citep{Han2021Aspartate,Zhou2009AspAT}. A second candidate, \textit{Zm00001eb231540}, annotated as L-aspartate oxidase, links aspartate to iminosuccinate and NAD biosynthesis, placing the Jarvis signal not only in the canonical methionine/threonine/lysine/isoleucine branch but also in an aspartate-dependent route tied to redox and energy metabolism \citep{Han2021Aspartate}. Jarvis FAA comparison also showed an increase in Asp fraction from 0.075 in Cycle~0 to 0.137 in Cycle~14, although this shift was nominally significant before multiple-testing correction and should therefore be interpreted cautiously. Even so, the direction of the FAA shift is consistent with the presence of multiple Jarvis candidates positioned in aspartate-family biosynthesis, Asp--Glu transamination, and Asp-derived NAD metabolism.

Indian Chief showed a complementary pattern. The broader $F_{ST}$ outlier set included \textit{Zm00001eb214360}, annotated as aspartate-semialdehyde dehydrogenase, which acts immediately downstream of aspartate kinase by converting aspartyl phosphate to aspartate-semialdehyde, a branching intermediate for methionine, threonine, lysine, and isoleucine biosynthesis \citep{Viola2001AspartatePathway,Wang2018AspSeeds}. The placement of \textit{ask2} in Jarvis and aspartate-semialdehyde dehydrogenase in Indian Chief is notable because these genes encode consecutive steps in the same conserved pathway. In Indian Chief, the FAA profile showed increased total FAA abundance and proline fraction, with reduced Asp and Glu fractions in Cycle~14. This pattern does not indicate simple aspartate accumulation, but instead suggests that selection may have shifted amino-acid N away from free Asp/Glu and toward downstream or alternative amino-acid pools, including proline and protein-turnover-derived FAA pools.

The Goodman--Buckler amino-acid GWAS added a grain-focused layer to this interpretation. Methionine and methionine-normalized traits identified \textit{Zm00001eb257910}, annotated as an aspartate aminotransferase/GOT-like gene. Since methionine is an aspartate-family amino acid, this AspAT/GOT signal supports a role for aspartate transamination in natural variation for grain amino-acid composition. This is consistent with rice data, where AspAT overexpression altered nitrogen metabolism and increased seed amino-acid and protein contents \citep{Zhou2009AspAT}. Thus, the FAA GWAS highlights an aspartate-related enzyme linked to a grain trait downstream of aspartate-family metabolism.

The soil-N environment GWAS provides the long-term ecological context for this pathway-level convergence. In contrast to the recurrent-selection scans, which capture allele-frequency changes, the soil-N GWAS reflects historical associations between maize landraces and the N environments from which they were collected. A key candidate from this layer was \textit{Zm00001eb220040}, an aspartate/glutamate/uridylate kinase domain-containing gene with aspartate kinase activity. This signal independently supports the involvement of aspartate-family entry steps in environmental N-associated variation. A second soil-N candidate, \textit{Zm00001eb005710}, annotated as an omega-amidase/NIT2-like CN hydrolase, is more directly related to Asn/Gln-linked C--N recycling and is discussed below with the Glu/Gln-centered candidates.

Together, these results highlight aspartate-family metabolism as a recurring convergence point for ecological adaptation, recurrent selection, and grain amino-acid variation. Although the specific candidate genes differ among analyses, their repeated placement in aspartate-family entry steps, downstream branch-point reactions, Asp-derived NAD metabolism, and Asp--Glu transamination suggests that modulation of aspartate-derived N flux is likely an important component of maize grain N remodeling.



\subsection{N transport and protein-turnover candidates recur across selection scans and grain amino-acid GWAS}

Beyond Asp-family metabolism, the candidate sets also highlight N transport, amino-acid transport, and protein turnover as recurring themes. These signals do not imply that the same loci were recovered across all datasets. Instead, they suggest that distinct candidates in different populations and mapping panels may affect related processes involved in N movement, amino-acid redistribution, proteostasis, and source--sink allocation.

In Indian Chief, \textit{Zm00001eb214890}, homologous to the Arabidopsis ammonium transporter \textit{AtAMT2}, may influence ammonium movement or redistribution \citep{Neuhaeuser2009AtAMT2}. Indian Chief also carried \textit{Zm00001eb287950}, an \textit{NRT1/PTR}-family candidate. Related transport signals were also detected in the Goodman--Buckler amino-acid GWAS, including \textit{Zm00001eb287980}, associated specifically with glutamine (\textit{Q}), and \textit{Zm00001eb277160}, associated with several asparagine- and aspartate-related ratio traits. The \textit{AVT3A}-like candidate \textit{Zm00001eb429100} was associated with glutamate (\textit{E}), alanine (\textit{A}), glycine (\textit{G}), and the LAV family composite. Because Arabidopsis AtAVT3 proteins mediate vacuolar amino-acid export, this candidate extends the transport signal to intracellular amino-acid redistribution \citep{Fujiki2017AtAVT3}.

Jarvis contributed a complementary set of amino-acid transport candidates, including \textit{Zm00001eb132750}, an LHT-like transporter related to a rice amino-acid transport family \citep{Fan2023LHT}; \textit{Zm00001eb159580}, a PROT-like transporter related to Arabidopsis proline/compatible-solute transporters \citep{Lehmann2011ProT}; and \textit{Zm00001eb389560}, a \textit{GLUTAMINE DUMPER}-family candidate linked to amino-acid export in Arabidopsis \citep{Pratelli2012GDU}. Together, these candidates suggest that both recurrent selection and natural grain amino-acid variation may involve changes in the movement of inorganic N and organic N among tissues, cells, and intracellular storage pools.

A second recurring theme was protein turnover and proteostasis. This connection was strongest in Indian Chief, where \textit{Zm00001eb352300}, homologous to Arabidopsis \textit{ATG18a}, points to autophagosome formation under nutrient stress and senescence \citep{Xiong2005ATG18a}. Indian Chief also contained \textit{Zm00001eb214780}, a senescence-specific cysteine protease related to \textit{SAG12}, which has been associated with N allocation during senescence and seed production \citep{James2018SAG12}. The localization of SAG12 to senescence-associated vacuoles with strong proteolytic activity further supports a role for senescence-related proteolysis in nutrient recycling \citep{Otegui2005SAV}. Consistent with this theme, the Goodman--Buckler GWAS identified candidates related to macroautophagy, ER-associated degradation, RING-type E3 ubiquitination, autophagosome assembly, and senescence regulation. Jarvis also contained \textit{Zm00001eb210720}, a 26S proteasome regulatory-particle candidate homologous to rice \textit{OsRpn5}, which is best interpreted as a proteostasis candidate rather than direct evidence for N remobilization \citep{Shibahara2002Proteasome}.

Together, the transport and protein-turnover candidates suggest mechanisms for N release, movement, and intracellular redistribution. However, these processes alone do not explain how released N is metabolically reassimilated. The Glu/Gln-related candidates provide this second layer, linking amino-acid turnover to C--N recycling and ammonium reassimilation.



\subsection{Glu/Gln metabolism links released amino-acid N with C--N recycling}

If protein turnover and amino-acid transport provide routes for releasing and moving organic N, then Glu/Gln metabolism provides a plausible route for processing that released nitrogen. In this layer, Indian Chief showed the clearest GDH-related signal. \textit{Zm00001eb060520} and \textit{Zm00001eb427810} are glutamate dehydrogenase candidates corresponding to maize \textit{gdh1} and \textit{gdh2}. Indian Chief also contained \textit{Zm00001eb162300}, annotated as an NADP-specific glutamate dehydrogenase 1-related protein. Plant GDH connects glutamate with 2-oxoglutarate, thereby linking amino-acid metabolism with central carbon metabolism. In Arabidopsis, NAD(H)-dependent GDH is not the main route for primary ammonium assimilation, but GDH mutants show reduced glutamate catabolism and altered amino-acid profiles under carbon limitation, supporting roles in amino-acid breakdown and metabolic recycling during stress \citep{Miyashita2008GDH,Dellero2020AminoNUE}.

The Goodman--Buckler amino-acid GWAS supports this same Glu/Gln-centered layer. Glutamine (\textit{Q}) was associated with \textit{Zm00001eb287980}, an \textit{NRT1/PTR FAMILY 3.1} transporter candidate, suggesting that natural variation in grain glutamine may involve N- or amino-acid-linked transport. Glutamate (\textit{E}) was associated with \textit{Zm00001eb429100}, an \textit{AVT3A}-like amino-acid transporter, extending the signal to intracellular amino-acid redistribution. In addition, the GWAS identified \textit{Zm00001eb201770}, a tyrosine aminotransferase candidate associated with \textit{D.IMNTDK}. Because aminotransferases commonly use 2-oxoglutarate as an amino-group acceptor, this candidate links amino-acid homeostasis to the same C--N interface represented by Glu, 2-oxoglutarate, and transamination reactions. The \textit{Total\_N}-associated GDH-related candidate \textit{Zm00001eb060520} provides an additional grain-focused connection between amino-acid-derived N and Glu/Gln metabolism. Together, these GWAS candidates extend the Glu/Gln signal beyond the recurrent-selection scans by implicating glutamine-associated transport, glutamate-associated intracellular transport, and 2-oxoglutarate-dependent transamination in natural variation for mature grain amino-acid composition.

A related C--N interface was identified in the soil-N GWAS through \textit{Zm00001eb005710}, annotated as an omega-amidase/NIT2-like CN hydrolase. Based on known omega-amidase biochemistry, this candidate is consistent with Asn/Gln-linked pathways that connect amino-acid catabolism to 2-oxoglutarate and oxaloacetate production from transamination-derived intermediates. Jarvis added a complementary transamination signal through \textit{Zm00001eb152450}, annotated as \textit{got1}/glutamate--oxaloacetate transaminase. This enzyme reversibly transfers amino groups among aspartate, 2-oxoglutarate, glutamate, and oxaloacetate, connecting Asp-family metabolism with Glu-centered C--N recycling. This is notable because \textit{Zm00001eb152450}/\textit{GOT1} was recently identified as \textit{THP3}, a teosinte-derived maize gene that enhances nitrogen assimilation, C--N balance, and seed protein accumulation \citep{Huang2026THP3}. Thus, the Jarvis \textit{got1} candidate links Asp remodeling to Glu/Gln-centered nitrogen recycling.

This Glu/Gln theme was further supported by \textit{Zm00001eb162900}, annotated as a GS catalytic domain-containing protein in Indian Chief. Glutamine synthetase incorporates ammonium into glutamine and is central to plant N assimilation and reassimilation. Because cereal GS isoforms are associated with grain development, nitrogen flux, and the provision of glutamine to developing grain \citep{Wei2021WheatGS,Fujita2022RiceGS13,Dellero2020AminoNUE}, the combination of GDH- and GS-like candidates suggests that Indian Chief differentiation may involve Glu/Gln cycling, Glu catabolism, ammonium release and reassimilation, and C--N metabolic adjustment rather than increased primary N assimilation alone.

A broader glutamine-dependent signal came from glutamine amidotransferase-domain candidates. In Indian Chief, \textit{Zm00001eb217220} encodes a glutamine amidotransferase domain-containing protein, and Jarvis contained a second candidate, \textit{Zm00001eb232460}. These genes should not be interpreted as direct Glu/Gln-cycle enzymes without functional validation, because glutamine amidotransferases participate in many biosynthetic pathways. However, their potential relevance to grain N metabolism is supported by rice \textit{FLOURY ENDOSPERM19} (\textit{FLO19}), a class I glutamine amidotransferase that affects grain filling, storage-substance accumulation, and C--N metabolism \citep{Lou2021FLO19}. More broadly, rice \textit{tdd1}, which encodes an anthranilate synthase beta-subunit that uses glutamine as an amide donor during tryptophan biosynthesis, illustrates how glutamine-dependent amidotransfer reactions can influence downstream developmental and metabolic outcomes \citep{Sazuka2009TDD1}. These comparisons are functional analogies rather than claims of direct homology or pathway equivalence.

Together, these results identify Glu/Gln metabolism as a metabolic bridge linking amino-acid release, ammonium reassimilation, C--N recycling, and grain N allocation. Rather than indicating a single causal gene, the candidate set suggests that recurrent selection, soil-N-related variation, and grain amino-acid GWAS repeatedly implicate GDH-, GS-, GOT-, omega-amidase/NIT2-like, glutamine transport, glutamate transport, and glutamine-dependent biosynthetic steps.


\subsection{Multi-trait GWAS candidates suggest regulatory and trafficking components of grain amino-acid variation}

Among the Goodman--Buckler amino-acid GWAS candidates, \textit{Zm00001eb393450} had the highest occurrence across significant trait associations, appearing in eight amino-acid or amino-acid-derived traits: alanine (\textit{A}), glutamate-total (\textit{E.Total}), \textit{EHPRQ}, branched-chain amino-acid traits (\textit{IVL} and \textit{LAV}), serine (\textit{S}), threonine (\textit{T}), and total amino-acid abundance. It is annotated as a transcriptional regulatory protein AlgP-like and also falls within a lysine-rich arabinogalactan-protein family. Its repeated association across multiple amino-acid traits makes it biologically compelling but must be viewed with caution. In cassava, an AlgP-annotated gene, \textit{Sc18g015190}, was identified by ionomic GWAS as a phosphorus-accumulation candidate, and promoter polymorphisms at this locus were associated with altered promoter activity, transcript abundance, and P accumulation \citep{Yin2022CassavaIonomics}. Functional silencing of \textit{Sc18g015190} further reduced chlorophyll and P content under low-P conditions, supporting a role for this AlgP-annotated gene in nutrient homeostasis \citep{Yin2022CassavaIonomics}.

A broader starvation-response context is supported by bacterial AlgP studies, where AlgP acts as a DNA-binding, histone H1-like protein linked to polyphosphate granule organization during nitrogen starvation \citep{Chawla2022AlgPPolyP}. Polyphosphate itself provides relevant nutrient-stress context as an ancient phosphate- and energy-storage polymer involved in stress responses and cellular regulation \citep{Achbergerova2011PolyP}. Although this bacterial function should not be directly transferred to maize, it conceptually supports the idea that AlgP-like or lysine-rich regulatory proteins may contribute to nutrient-linked cellular organization and stress responses. In the Goodman--Buckler panel, the association of \textit{Zm00001eb393450} with multiple amino-acid traits suggests that this locus may influence broad variation in grain amino-acid balance through regulatory or cellular-homeostasis mechanisms rather than a single biosynthetic reaction. Thus, \textit{Zm00001eb393450}, together with other recurrent trafficking-related GWAS candidates, provides a useful target set for future validation.

Beyond the AlgP-like gene, GWAS identified candidates annotated for Golgi or anterograde transport, Golgin-like function, and nucleotide-sugar transport, including UDP-galactose/glucose transporter-like genes. One example was \textit{Zm00001eb137680}, a Golgin candidate associated with five amino-acid traits (\textit{IVL}, \textit{S}, \textit{V}, \textit{W}, and \textit{Y}). In Arabidopsis, \textit{MAG4}/\textit{Atp115}, a Golgi-localized tethering factor, is required for efficient ER-to-Golgi transport of seed storage-protein precursors, and \textit{mag4} mutants accumulate 12S globulin and 2S albumin precursors in dry seeds \citep{Takahashi2010MAG4}. Thus, although these maize GWAS candidates require validation, their recurrence across multiple amino-acid traits supports grain amino-acid variation reflecting not only biosynthetic-enzyme differences but also trafficking processes. Additional candidate-gene annotations, trait associations, and model-specific GWAS results are available through the Shiny application at \url{https://j83gk2-nirwan-tandukar.shinyapps.io/amino_gwas_app/}.


\subsection{Proline remodeling and a shared Pro/Asp QTL implicate amino-acid pool partitioning}

Proline showed one of the clearest amino-acid phenotypes in the recurrent-selection populations. In Indian Chief, Cycle~14 had an expanded total FAA pool and a strongly enriched proline fraction, while Asp and Glu fractions declined. This pattern indicates that selection shifted the balance of soluble amino-acid pools rather than uniformly increasing all FAA components. Because proline can act as a stress-linked amino acid and a flexible N-containing metabolite, its enrichment may reflect changes in amino-acid storage, turnover-derived FAA accumulation, or source--sink N partitioning. However, we interpret this pattern cautiously, as the Indian Chief result alone does not reveal the biochemical pathway underlying proline enrichment or demonstrate increased total grain N remobilization.

The B64 $\times$ CML14 mapping result provides an independent genetic link to this proline-centered pattern. Although B64 and CML14 were selected for contrasting grain asparagine rather than proline, the biparental scan identified a shared chromosome~3 QTL for free proline and aspartate, with both traits peaking at the same marker. This overlap suggests that Pro and Asp variation can share genetic control in at least one segregating background, even though they represent different biochemical axes. Together with the Indian Chief FAA shift, this supports the idea that proline remodeling is part of the broader grain N-remodeling network rather than an isolated phenotype.

A nearby candidate gene at the shared Pro/Asp QTL peak, \textit{Zm00001eb127670}, encodes a GTP-binding protein SAR1A, a small GTPase that initiates COPII vesicle formation and mediates ER-to-Golgi protein export. In rice endosperm, simultaneous knockdown of \textit{OsSar1a/b/c} disrupts storage-protein trafficking, causing proglutelin accumulation in the ER, reduced mature glutelin, altered $\alpha$-globulin deposition, and floury/shrunken seeds \citep{Tian2013Sar1}. Thus, although \textit{SAR1A} is not a canonical proline or aspartate biosynthetic enzyme, its location at the shared Pro/Asp QTL peak suggests a plausible connection between endomembrane trafficking, storage-protein deposition, and free amino-acid balance in maize kernels.

This candidate was not in the top 5\% $F_{ST}$ outlier set in either recurrent-selection population. In Indian Chief, the window containing \textit{Zm00001eb127670} had an $F_{ST}$ of 0.07329, below the 5\% cutoff of 0.15684. In Jarvis, however, the same gene was nearly at the threshold, with an $F_{ST}$ of 0.17827 versus a 5\% cutoff of 0.17831. Thus, although \textit{Zm00001eb127670} was not formally classified as an $F_{ST}$ outlier, its near-threshold differentiation in Jarvis supports considering \textit{SAR1A} as a candidate linking the shared Pro/Asp QTL with storage-protein trafficking and amino-acid pool remodeling.

Overall, the \textit{SAR1A} signal supports the central conclusion that grain amino-acid variation reflects not only biosynthesis but also protein trafficking, turnover, and nitrogen redistribution during kernel development. This trafficking candidate may affect free Pro and Asp indirectly by altering how amino acids are partitioned between soluble and storage-protein-bound pools in the endosperm. Fine-mapping and developing-kernel expression analysis will be needed to test whether variation at \textit{Zm00001eb127670} underlies the shared Pro/Asp QTL.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec13}
%Sarah is still working on this 

By integrating soil N environmental GWAS, recurrent-selection population genomics, grain amino-acid GWAS, and biparental QTL mapping, we show that maize grain nitrogen composition reflects coordinated variation across interconnected biological pathways rather than control by a small number of major-effect loci. 

\quad Across independent datasets, we identify repeated involvement of four core processes: (1) aspartate-family metabolism, which governs allocation of nitrogen into essential amino-acid biosynthetic pathways; (2) Glu/Gln-centered C--N cycling, linking nitrogen assimilation and reassimilation with central carbon metabolism; (3) amino-acid transport and intracellular partitioning; and (4) protein turnover and recycling through autophagy- and ubiquitin-mediated pathways. Convergence on these processes is supported by (1) GWAS signals for amino-acid transporters, dehydrogenases, and Asp-family enzymes, (2) soil-N associations implicating asparagine and aspartate metabolism in environmental adaptation, (3) selection-driven FAA remodeling and population-specific differentiation in Indian Chief and Jarvis, and (4) a shared Pro/Asp QTL implicating ER-to-Golgi trafficking. 

\quad Importantly, the recurrent-selection populations demonstrate that substantial shifts in grain amino-acid composition can arise through modest allele-frequency changes distributed across these pathways. Indian Chief exhibited expansion of the FAA pool and strong proline enrichment with reduced Asp and Glu fractions, coupled with differentiation in genes related to proteolysis, autophagy, and Glu/Gln metabolism. In contrast, Jarvis showed weaker phenotypic shifts but contained candidate loci directly linked to aspartate-family biosynthesis. These contrasting outcomes indicate that similar selection pressures can reshape grain nitrogen allocation through distinct genetic routes within a shared metabolic network. 
Together, these results support a model in which maize grain nitrogen variation is governed by distributed modulation of N flux, protein turnover, and intracellular transport during kernel development. In this framework, free and protein-bound amino-acid pools are not passive storage forms but dynamic products of coordinated metabolic and cellular processes. Perturbation of aspartate-family enzymes should shift the balance of nitrogen among downstream amino-acid pools; manipulation of glutamate dehydrogenase, glutamine synthetase, or aminotransferases should alter C--N recycling and FAA accumulation; and disruption of protein trafficking or autophagy pathways should affect the partitioning between free and storage-protein amino acids. Future functional studies targeting these processes will be critical to resolving causal mechanisms. 

\quad From an applied perspective, our findings suggest that reducing grain nitrogen content while maintaining agronomic performance will require coordinated modification of multiple pathways rather than targeting a single locus. Candidate targets include regulators of Asp-family flux, Glu/Gln cycling enzymes, amino-acid transporters, and components of protein turnover and trafficking systems. By identifying these convergent processes, this study provides a framework for breeding and engineering strategies aimed at improving nitrogen use efficiency and mitigating nitrogen losses in maize production systems{Cite CERCA Paper Here}.




\section{Methods}\label{methods}

\subsection{Germplasm used}
\subsubsection{Maize Landraces}
The maize landraces discussed by Romero Navarro et al. (2017, ref) come from the CIMMYT International Germplasm Bank collection, which contains over 24,000 accessions of traditional maize varieties primarily from North, Central, and South America. They characterized a core collection of 3,511 genotypes based on availability of accurate geographic coordinates and complete environmental data for their collection locations. These landraces are genotyped using genotyping-by-sequencing (GBS) at about 2X coverage, with SNP calling and imputation for analysis. The population represents a geographically and genetically diverse panel that serves as an important resource for studying environmental adaptation like soil nitrogen (N) availability.
How were they grown when they were phenotyped?

\subsubsection{Indian Chief and Jarvis (IC and J)  }
We worked with two open-pollinated maize populations Indian Chief (IC) and Jarvis (J) from Moll \textit{et al.}~\citep{Moll1994} that underwent reciprocal recurrent selection for 14 generations for grain yield. Beginning from base populations (G$_0$), each cycle involved intercrossing the best families selected on hybrid (IC$\times$J) performance and advancing through a selfed recombination nursery.

\subsubsection{Indian Chief and Jarvis recurrent-selection populations}

We used two open-pollinated maize populations, Indian Chief (IC) and Jarvis Golden Prolific (J), from the recurrent-selection experiment described by \citet{Moll1994}. These populations underwent 14 cycles of reciprocal recurrent selection for grain yield. In this scheme, parents within each population were selected based on the performance of their hybrid progeny in the IC~$\times$~J population, and selfed progeny from the selected parents were intermated to generate the next selection cycle. For the present study, individuals from the base populations, Cycle~0 (C0), and the selected populations, Cycle~14 (C14), were analyzed for both Indian Chief and Jarvis.

Plants were grown using a \textit{[insert experimental design; e.g., randomized complete block design]} with \textit{[insert number]} biological replicates per population--cycle group. Standard agronomic management was applied throughout the season, including \textit{[insert planting date, spacing, fertilizer regime, irrigation/rainfed condition, and pest/weed management if relevant]}. Tissue for free amino acid profiling was collected from \textit{[insert tissue; e.g., grain, leaf, seedling tissue]} at \textit{[insert developmental stage or harvest time]}, immediately frozen or dried according to the extraction protocol, and stored at \textit{[insert storage condition]} until metabolite analysis.


\subsubsection{The Goodman-Buckler Maize Association Panel}
The Goodman-Buckler maize association panel (Flint-Garcia et al., 2005)  was grown at Genetics Farm near Columbia Missouri in 2017 and 2018 in two replicates each, in a randomized complete block design. Of the 282 lines in this panel, only 279 were grown successfully. 13 kernels of each line were planted in a 10 foot row and self-pollinated. At full physiological maturity, the pollinated ears were harvested, husk leaves were removed, and were subsequently dried at 105 degrees fahrenheit and <20 relative humidity for 5 days to remove moisture from the grain. Ears from each line in each rep and year were shelled and bulked to form four representative composite grain samples for each of the 279 lines. For phenotypic evaluation, 25 random seeds were selected from each bulked sample to be ground and prepared for amino acid extraction and quantification

\subsubsection{Bi-parental Mapping Population}
The bi-parental QTL mapping population was developed by crossing the two inbred lines of the Goodman-Buckler Association Panel with the lowest and highest free Asn accumulation in the grain, B64 and CML14, respectively. F2:3 families were grown in two complete randomized blocks in 2024 at Genetics Farm near Columbia Missouri. For each plot, 13 kernels were planted in a 10-foot row and sib-pollinated to produce three ears. At full physiological maturity, the pollinated ears were harvested, husk leaves were removed, and ears were subsequently dried at 41 degrees Celsius and 20\% relative humidity for 5 days to remove moisture from the grain. From each ear, 10 representative kernels were sampled then bulked, ground, and prepared for amino acid extraction and quantification.


\subsection{Phenotypic Evaluation}
\subsubsection{Grain/Stover Nitrogen}

Grain and stover N concentrations for all Indian Chief (IC) and Jarvis (J) lines were previously determined for the base generation (G$_0$) and the 14th cycle of selection (G$_{14}$) by Moll \textit{et al.}~\citep{Moll1994}. At a high fertilizer rate of $224~\mathrm{kg~N~ha^{-1}}$, J$_{14}$ produced heavier kernels and had higher grain N concentration than the original J$_0$ line. Post-anthesis N uptake from the soil supplied nearly all of this additional grain N, whereas the amount of N remobilized from stover to grain remained largely unchanged.

Indian Chief showed a contrasting response. In IC$_{14}$, vegetative biomass increased, but grain weight and grain N concentration remained similar to IC$_0$. Loss of N from stover after flowering was lower than in the base generation, indicating that more N was retained in stems and leaves and less was transferred to the grain. Thus, 14 cycles of selection increased grain N in Jarvis primarily through greater post-anthesis N uptake, whereas Indian Chief retained more N in vegetative tissue and showed little change in grain N concentration~\citep{Moll1994}.


\subsubsection{Soil Nitrogen}
The analyzed phenotypes include total N in the soil (TN) and nitrate deposition (NHx, which includes NH3 and NH4+). N data were derived from the top 0-5 cm of the soil, representing the active root zone for nutrient uptake, using ISRIC data sets (International Soil Reference and Information Center). Historical nitrate deposition data (NHx) from 1983 was also sourced from ISRIC to understand its relationship with nutrient uptake. 

\subsubsection{Amino Acid Profiling}
PBAAs were extracted and quantified as described in Yobi and Angelovici (2018). This protocol utilizes microscale protein hydrolysis and absolute quantification using multiple reaction monitoring-based liquid chromatography–tandem mass spectrometry detection. During acid hydrolysis, Gln and Asn are converted into Glu and Asp, respectively. Therefore, we denote both Gln and Glu as Glx and Asn and Asp as Asx. During the extraction, Trp and Cys were fully destroyed and Gly was poorly detected, therefore these three amino acids were excluded from the dataset. In total, 15 PBAAs were analyzed to represent 17 PBAAs. FAAs were extracted and quantified as described in (Gu et al., 2007). Unlike the protocol above, all 20 proteinogenic amino acids were independently and successfully detected using UPLC-MS/MS.


\subsection{Sequencing and genotyping}

\subsubsection{Indian Chief and Jarvis}

Genomic DNA was isolated from young leaf tissue of individuals from four reciprocal recurrent-selection population--cycle groups: Indian Chief base generation (I01), Indian Chief Cycle~14 (I14), Jarvis base generation (J01), and Jarvis Cycle~14 (J14). Each group initially included 96 individuals, for a total of 384 samples. These populations were generated after 14 cycles of reciprocal recurrent selection for increased grain yield \citep{Moll1994}. Paired-end Illumina libraries were constructed and sequenced as 2~$\times$~150~bp reads on a NovaSeq~6000 platform. Raw base-call files were demultiplexed with \texttt{fqtk} v0.3.1, yielding one 96-sample plate for each population--cycle group (Supplementary Table~S1).

Adapter trimming and quality filtering used \texttt{fastp} v0.23.4, and read quality was assessed with \texttt{FastQC} v0.12.1. Trimmed reads were aligned to the B73 NAM reference genome RefGen\_v5 (GCF\_902167145.1) with \texttt{BWA-MEM} v0.7.18. Alignments were coordinate-sorted, duplicates were collapsed with \texttt{Picard MarkDuplicates} v3.1.1, and read groups were standardized with \texttt{Picard AddOrReplaceReadGroups}. Variant discovery followed the GATK Best Practices joint-genotyping workflow using \texttt{GATK} v4.5.0.0. Joint genotyping was performed per chromosome with \texttt{CombineGVCFs} and \texttt{GenotypeGVCFs}, generating 10 per-chromosome VCFs that were merged into a cohort VCF with 80,728,251 raw variant sites. Five low-quality samples (I14\_A07, I14\_B06, I14\_D05, I14\_F12, J14\_H11) were removed, leaving 379 genomes for population-genomic analysis (Supplementary Table~S1). Genome-wide coverage was estimated with \texttt{Picard CollectWgsMetrics}: mean coverage was 0.28$\times$ for I01, 0.38$\times$ for I14, 0.32$\times$ for J01, and 0.37$\times$ for J14, consistent with low-pass skim sequencing (Supplementary Fig.~S1). SNPs and indels were filtered with GATK hard-filtering criteria, yielding 59,199,563 high-confidence variants, including 52,543,238 SNPs and 6,656,325 indels across the 10 maize chromosomes and scaffolds (Supplementary Table~S2). The filtered SNP set was used for downstream population-genomic analyses.


\subsubsection{The Goodman-Buckler maize association panel}
The Goodman-Buckler maize association panel had been previously genotyped with both the Illumina MaizeSNP50 BeadChip (Cook et al., 2012) and with genotyping-by-sequencing (GBS) method (Elshire et al., 2011) as described in (Lipka et al., (2013). We filtered SNPs by minor allele frequency greater than 0.05 prior to analysis. HAPPI GWAS utilizes GAPIT MLMM, FarmCPU, and BLINK models to conduct univariate GWAS. Following the GWAS, we used false discovery rate (FDR) to correct the multiple hypothesis testing problem at 5\%. Using the Maize AGP\_V5, we identified candidate genes within 25kb on either side of SNPs with a p value of less than 0.001. 

\subsubsection{Bi-parental Mapping Population}
Leaf tissues were collected from F2 plants and lyophillized prior to DNA extraction and genotyping using the mid-density temperate corn panel by Agriplex Genomics (Cleveland, OH).

\subsection{Population-genomic analyses}

\subsubsection{Genotype-likelihood framework}

Population-genomic statistics were estimated using ANGSD v0.941 \citep{Korneliussen2014}, which is appropriate for low-coverage sequencing because it uses genotype likelihoods rather than hard genotype calls. Analyses were performed separately for Indian Chief Cycle~0 (IC\_C0), Indian Chief Cycle~14 (IC\_C14), Jarvis Cycle~0 (J\_C0), and Jarvis Cycle~14 (J\_C14). The same read- and base-level filtering parameters were applied across all population--cycle groups to ensure comparable estimates among contrasts.

For each population--cycle group, site allele-frequency likelihoods were estimated with ANGSD and stored as SAF files. Folded one-dimensional site-frequency spectra were inferred with \texttt{realSFS} for within-population analyses, because a reliable ancestral allele state was not assumed. For pairwise analyses, joint two-dimensional site-frequency spectra were estimated from the corresponding SAF files using \texttt{winsfs}, a window-EM SFS estimator, to improve computational efficiency. These joint spectra were then used as priors for downstream $F_{ST}$ estimation.

\subsubsection{Population differentiation using $F_{ST}$}

Genetic differentiation between ancestral and selected cycles was quantified using the ANGSD/\texttt{realSFS} $F_{ST}$ workflow. Pairwise $F_{ST}$ was estimated separately for the IC\_C0 versus IC\_C14 and J\_C0 versus J\_C14 contrasts. For each contrast, the joint two-dimensional site-frequency spectrum was first estimated using \texttt{winsfs} from the corresponding SAF files and then supplied as the prior to \texttt{realSFS fst index}. Windowed $F_{ST}$ values were summarized across the genome using 100~kb sliding windows with a 10~kb step size. The top 1\% of windows from each contrast were treated as high-differentiation outliers and retained for downstream candidate-region annotation.

\subsubsection{Nucleotide diversity}

Within-population nucleotide diversity was estimated separately for IC\_C0, IC\_C14, J\_C0, and J\_C14. Population-scaled theta estimates were computed from the folded one-dimensional site-frequency spectrum using \texttt{realSFS saf2theta}. Nucleotide diversity ($\pi$) was then summarized across the genome using \texttt{thetaStat do\_stat}. Windowed $\pi$ estimates were calculated in 100~kb sliding windows with a 10~kb step size, matching the windowing scheme used for the $F_{ST}$ scans. Windows showing strong reductions in nucleotide diversity in Cycle~14 relative to Cycle~0 were retained as candidate regions consistent with selection-associated loss of variation.

\subsubsection{Tajima's $D$}

Tajima's $D$ was estimated for each population--cycle group using the same theta-based ANGSD workflow. Folded one-dimensional site-frequency spectra were used to compute theta estimates with \texttt{realSFS saf2theta}, and Tajima's $D$ was summarized with \texttt{thetaStat do\_stat}. Values were calculated in 100~kb sliding windows with a 10~kb step size. Windows with extreme Tajima's $D$ values were retained as candidate regions reflecting departures from neutral allele-frequency expectations, including possible selection-associated shifts in the site-frequency spectrum.

\subsubsection{Candidate window annotation}

Candidate windows from the $F_{ST}$, nucleotide diversity, and Tajima's $D$ analyses were intersected with the maize reference gene annotation \textit{[insert annotation version]}. Genes overlapping or located near candidate windows were retained as candidate genes. Candidate lists from the three statistics were then compared within Indian Chief and Jarvis to identify genes supported by multiple population-genomic signals.

\subsubsection{Genome-wide association Studies}
\paragraph{Environment GWAS}
GWAS was performed on maize landraces (n = 3,511) using four GWAS models: MLM, MLMM, BLINK and FarmCPU. Approximately 495,755 SNPs for maize landraces. The maize genotype was based on the Zm-B73-REFERENCE-NAM-5.0 genome assembly. Candidate genes located within 25 kb maize landraces were identified using the MaizeGDB, Phytozome, and Arabidopsis databases. 

\paragraph{Metabolite GWAS}
Using the same pipeline described above for the envGWAS, we performed metabolite GWAS using the Goodman-Buckler Association Panel (n-278). In total, XXX SNPs were used to evaluate 110 traits for FAAs and 77 traits for PBAAs including absolute composition, total composition, relative composition, and various biochemical ratios. Prior to analysis, best linear unbiased predictions or BLUPs were calculated for each trait. For the metabolite GWAS, we used a GAPIT pipeline based on Holistic Analysis with Pre- and Post-Integration, or HAPPI GWAS. This pipeline, as described in (Slaten et al., 2020), removes outliers, optimizes transformation, and calculates BLUPs prior to the association study. 

To estimate the nitrogen content of the Goodman-Buckler maize association panel, we calculated the respective percentage of mass occupied by nitrogen in the 20 proteinogenic amino acids. Across the 4 composite samples, we multiplied the quantity of each amino acid in $\mu\mathrm{g}/\mathrm{mg}$ by the calculated \% nitrogen mass for that amino acid to solve for the  quantity of nitrogen in $\mu\mathrm{g}/\mathrm{mg}$. We added the totals for each of the 20 proteinogenic amino acids together for the free amino acids as well as the quantified protein bound amino acids to find total approximated nitrogen content in each composite sample. 

\[
\%N_X =
\left(
\frac{n_{N,X}\times M_N}{M_X}
\right)\times 100
\]

where,
\[
M_X = \text{molar mass of amino acid } X
\]

\[
M_N = \text{molar mass of nitrogen}
\]

\[
n_{N,X} = \text{number of nitrogen atoms in amino acid } X
\]

Finally,
\[
N_X\;(\mu g/mg) =
\left(
\frac{n_{N,X}\times M_N}{M_X}
\right)
\times AA_X\;(\mu g/mg)
\]



We used these total proxy nitrogen (TPN) amounts for each composite sample to calculate the Best Linear Unbiased prediction (BLUP) before performing the GWAS as described above. We used a false discovery rate (FDR) to correct the multiple hypothesis testing problem at 5\%. Using the MaizeMine tool on MaizeGDB, we identified candidate genes within 100kb on either side of SNPs with a p value of less than 0.001. 

\paragraph{Quantitative Trait Loci Mapping}
We began with 2490 markers in total and following filtering of failed, monomorphic, and heterozygous markers 611 were used for the analysis. Markers were re-coded to be compatible with the r/qtl package used for mapping. Each allele was re-coded to AA to correspond with the allele associated with B64, BB for CML14, and AB if heterozygous. Best Linear Unbiased Predictions of absolute free aspartate content were calculated and used to construct the genetic map. To ensure quality of the genetic map, markers with more than 10\% inconclusive genotyping results were excluded and segregation ratios were checked to identify and remedy potential switched alleles. Linkage groups were formed by calculating max recombination fraction at 0.35 and minimum LOD at 15. Ultimately, one linkage group required manual separation and subsequent re-calculation of recombination fraction and LOD to produce an accurate genetic map to reflect the 10 chromosomes of maize. QTL scans were performed and significance thresholds were established at a=0.05 using 1000 permutations and Haley-Knott regression.
